This book is about language, specifically a systems programming language called Ad1, but also about human language and celebrating the great works of human civilization. For millenia humans have endeavored to describe their world, from flat, to round, to beyond. Similarly, Ad1 is a language for describing a discrete, circular world and organizing great enterprises there.

Bigtalk

The ℝeal world is infinite in space, complexly interconnected, and evolves through the arrow of time; while computers are finite in memory, have only a few actors, and proceed incrementally through time. Then, programming is the art of depicting the real world in finite digital computers. We model war, science, economics, relationships, and we navigate, drive, automate, even fly in space! And while a program may live in computer memory, its relationship to the world lives in programmers' minds and our imagination.

This mental mapping, from real to discrete, is difficult and requires all the techniques for managing complexity and communicating values and operations found in other professional fields in science, math, and engineering. Further, professional programmers are not insulated from the real world and we have (varying levels of) responsibility to learn from the great works and great mistakes of history. A good programming environment helps.

Written text, with occasional object and control flow diagrams, is still the best media for projecting our abstract views onto computer memory, for the same reasons written speech and mathematics has been used for millenias, namely:

  1. Written language forces you to serialize and organize your thoughts.
  2. Words and symbols can change and grow in meaning with their context.
  3. From a finite set of sounds we can form endless words, whose meanings are not fixed but evolve over time.
To demonstrate the power of written speech, the two of the greatest calls to action in modern western civilization are reproduced at the end of this chapter.

There is an additional symmetry, why a painting of the real world in memory should use written text as medium: spoken language is naturally serialized and self-referencing, just as processors execute machine words sequentially and procedurally. As the objects and ideas in real life are limitless, so are the symbols in human language. As the memory and operations in a computer are finite, so are the words in its instruction set. Just as programmers map real to discrete, so do programming languages map an infinite set of symbolic expressions to finite instructions.

Finally, it is common for books about languages to emphasize some programming paradigm, and I like the idea of literate programming. You are in command and control of memory and I/O, but you should be careful to describe what it means through text and by evolving your language with new types, concepts, and transformations. There should also be a way to embed simple figures in code. Bigtalk is having command of all the processors and being articulate about their memories. It becomes an addiction; Napoleon said, ``I love power. But it is as an artist that I love it. I love it as a mussician loves his violin, to draw out its sounds and chords and harmonies.''

Why Another Language?

Ad1 is yet another systems-level programming language; its base operators map directly to processor instructions being represented and executed on the silicon. Like any ancient city, today it builds on the foundations laid by many before, Shockley, Moore, Watson, Backus, McCarthy, Djkstra, Ritchie, Thomson, Kernigan, Stroustrup, David Hennesy, Alexander Stepanov, Paul Graham, etc. In particular Ad1 draws heavily from three prior languages: C for its unobstructed view of the machine and Linux, C++ for generic type concepts and the STL, and Lisp for designing an interactive compiler.

Although the genealogy of programming languages continues to grow more scattered and confused, at the same time hardware architecture has been converging since the the early days of C. Ad1 can now assume a little endian machine, a downward growing procedural call stack, two's complement arithmetic, some UTF-8 support, word sizes sufficient to address memory, and resources for more a complex compiler.

There have also been many new ideas incorporated in computer languages, and some have been judiciously borrowed for Ad1, including transposable struct ✗ array combinations, type concepts from Elements of Programming by Stepanov, linear/transactional types inspired by Girard and Rust, for helping track memory management, natural numbers from digital signal processing, inheritence through symbols, s-like compiler expressions from Lisp, etc. But not including text preprocessor, std malloc() interface, RAII, inheritence through object class trees, not really exceptions, and not exclusively prefix notation.
